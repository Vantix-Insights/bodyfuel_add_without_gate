What Is BMI and Why Does It Matter?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value calculated from your height and weight. It was developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s as a quick screening tool to identify whether a person may have a weight-related health risk. BMI does not directly measure body fat, but studies have shown that it correlates with more direct measures of body fatness, such as underwater weighing.

The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies BMI for adults 20 years and older as follows:

  • Below 18.5 — Underweight. May indicate malnutrition, eating disorders, or other health problems.
  • 18.5 to 24.9 — Normal weight. Associated with the lowest risk of cardiovascular disease and mortality.
  • 25.0 to 29.9 — Overweight. Increased risk of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and heart disease.
  • 30.0 and above — Obese. Substantially elevated risk of multiple chronic diseases.

Important limitations: BMI does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass. A highly muscular athlete may be classified as "overweight" despite having very low body fat. Similarly, an older adult with low muscle mass may register a "normal" BMI while carrying excess fat. Use BMI alongside other measures such as waist circumference, body fat percentage, and blood markers for a more complete picture.

How BMI Is Calculated

Metric: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²
Imperial: BMI = (weight (lbs) × 703) ÷ height (in)²

Example: A person weighing 75 kg at 175 cm has a BMI of 75 ÷ (1.75)² = 24.5 — Normal weight.

What Are Calories and How Many Do You Need?

A calorie (technically a kilocalorie, or kcal) is a unit of energy. Your body needs a certain number of calories each day just to survive — to keep your heart beating, lungs breathing, brain functioning, and cells repairing themselves. This baseline need is called your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR).

Beyond your BMR, you burn additional calories through physical activity, digestion (the thermic effect of food), and daily movement like walking. The sum of all these is your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE).

  • To lose fat: consume fewer calories than your TDEE (a "calorie deficit"). A deficit of 500 kcal/day typically produces about 0.5 kg (1 lb) of fat loss per week.
  • To maintain weight: match your calorie intake to your TDEE.
  • To build muscle: consume slightly more than your TDEE (a "calorie surplus"). A surplus of 200–300 kcal/day supports muscle growth while minimising fat gain.

The Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (Most Accurate)

Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Then: TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier
(Sedentary × 1.2 · Lightly Active × 1.375 · Moderately Active × 1.55 · Very Active × 1.725 · Athlete × 1.9)

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation, published in 1990, is considered the most accurate formula for most people. A 2005 study in the Journal of the American Dietetic Association found it predicted resting energy expenditure within 10% in 82% of participants — outperforming the older Harris-Benedict equation revised in 1984.

If you enter your body fat percentage, this calculator switches to the Katch-McArdle formula, which calculates BMR from lean body mass instead of total weight. This is more accurate for lean athletes and muscular individuals.

Understanding Your Macros

Macronutrients are the three major categories of nutrients that provide energy: protein, carbohydrates, and dietary fat. Each gram provides a fixed number of calories:

  • Protein — 4 kcal per gram. Builds and repairs muscle tissue, supports immune function, and keeps you full longer than carbs or fat.
  • Carbohydrates — 4 kcal per gram. Your body's preferred fuel source for high-intensity exercise and brain function. Includes starches, sugars, and dietary fibre.
  • Dietary Fat — 9 kcal per gram. Essential for hormone production, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), and cell membrane integrity. Fat is energy-dense but critical — do not eliminate it.

Recommended Macro Ratios by Goal

Fat Loss (Cutting): Protein 35% · Carbs 35% · Fat 30%
Maintenance: Protein 25% · Carbs 45% · Fat 30%
Muscle Building (Bulking): Protein 30% · Carbs 45% · Fat 25%

These ratios follow guidelines from the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) and the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN).

For the most accurate protein target, many sports dietitians recommend 1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg of body weight per day for individuals engaged in resistance training, regardless of other macros. This calculator automatically reflects this principle in the goal-based ratios above.

Daily Water Intake: Why It's Calculated Alongside Calories

Adequate hydration directly affects metabolism, appetite control, and workout performance. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism found that drinking 500 ml of water temporarily increased metabolic rate by approximately 30% for 30–40 minutes.

This calculator uses the weight-based hydration formula: approximately 35 ml of water per kilogram of body weight per day for moderately active adults, adjusted upward for higher activity levels. This aligns with recommendations from the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA). Note that total water includes all beverages and water content in food — roughly 20% of daily water needs come from solid foods.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I recalculate my calories?
Recalculate every 4–6 weeks, or whenever your weight changes by more than 3–4 kg. As you lose fat, your BMR decreases, so your calorie target needs to adjust downward to maintain the same rate of loss. Most people hit a plateau because they don't account for this.
Is 1200 calories too low? What is the minimum safe intake?
For most adults, eating below 1200 kcal/day (women) or 1500 kcal/day (men) is considered a very-low-calorie diet (VLCD) and should only be done under medical supervision. Eating too little causes muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, metabolic adaptation, fatigue, and hormonal disruption. A moderate deficit of 300–500 kcal/day below TDEE is safer and more sustainable.
Can I build muscle and lose fat at the same time?
"Body recomposition" — gaining muscle while losing fat — is possible, but primarily for beginners, those returning after a long break, and individuals with a higher body fat percentage. For experienced lifters, it's very slow. The most efficient approach is to alternate focused cutting and bulking phases.
Why is my calorie number different from other calculators?
Different calculators use different formulas. The two most common are Harris-Benedict (1984 revision) and Mifflin-St Jeor (1990). This calculator uses Mifflin-St Jeor, which the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics considers the most accurate for most people. If you entered body fat percentage, it uses the even more precise Katch-McArdle formula.
How accurate is this calculator?
Predictive equations like Mifflin-St Jeor have an inherent margin of error of roughly ±10%. This is because individual metabolisms vary based on genetics, hormones, gut microbiome, sleep quality, and stress. Use the result as a starting point, track your weight for 2–3 weeks, and adjust calories by 100–150 kcal if your weight is not moving in the expected direction.
